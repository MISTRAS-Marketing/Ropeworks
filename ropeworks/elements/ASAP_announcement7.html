The Back-up Device Dilemma: The MISTRAS Ropeworks Team Switches from the Shunt to ASAP


Hello Friends,

In January 2012, Petzl released a statement which withdrew its earlier support for the use of the Shunt as a rope access back-up device. Petzl‘s main concern is the Shunt can be defeated by the user when used with a tow string and lanyard.

The Shunt has been the most widely-used rope access back-up device since the late 1980’s. Despite 18 years of incident-free use as our primary back-up device, Ropeworks began evaluating alternatives immediately following the announcement. We found some merit in Petzl’s concerns and we could not justify the continued use of a device expressly against the manufacturer’s recommendations.

In May 2012, we transitioned to using the ASAP as the primary back-up device in our rope access training courses. Implementation into our field operations is underway.

We approached this change thoughtfully and deliberately. In March, we brought together seven of our Field Team Leaders and Instructors to evaluate several of the leading alternatives to the Shunt. We identified the Petzl ASAP as a viable option and decided to perform further evaluations in the field and some independent drop testing.

In April, in cooperation with Petzl, we performed a series of drop tests with the ASAP, simulating various rope access scenarios found in field and training environments. We captured the tests on video found on our website:

ASAP Demonstrations at Petzl America

Our sample sizes of drops were too small to be considered statistically significant. It was a compelling demonstration of the ASAP’s consistent performance and highlighted a few limitations. Here are some of our key conclusions:

• Having the ASAP positioned directly above an obstacle (e.g., I’D, handle ascender or knot)
does not detrimentally affect its function or performance.

• Locking or engaging the ASAP on rope is one way to minimize potential free fall distances
when working statically on rope. This practice, however, is likely to accelerate rope wear. A
better option is to tie a knot (or slip knot) directly below the ASAP to maintain the device in a
high position. In windy conditions, tying a knot can also help prevent a slack loop from forming
above the device.

• Fall distance was very consistent regardless of the fall factor and less than other devices we
tested.

• When monitored appropriately, the ASAP can be connected to the anchor and safely used as a back-up device in a vertical hauling scenario (e.g., pitch-head haul).

• The ASAP is designed to engage at (or just over) 2.5 m/s. It does not protect against low
velocity vertical falls, uncontrolled descent, or low velocity swing falls (low angle horizontal
travel) at speeds below 2.5 m/s. When exposed to these conditions, the user must mitigate
the situation appropriately. Tying a well-placed knot in the safety line below the ASAP is an
effective method of mitigating the low velocity swing fall hazard.

• Due to the potential for creating an exceptionally high impact force, minimize time spent with
two ASAPs connected to the same line (passing an obstacle).

• When using one ASAP per person in a two person load situation, it is optimal to connect to
two individual ropes rather than one.

• Low clearance situations require special attention for all back-up devices. Each scenario is
different and needs to be mitigated appropriately.

• Performing a thorough risk assessment is essential for all rope access equipment.

• Appropriate training is required for the safe use of all rope access equipment.

We were satisfied with the results of our simulated skills test and the drop simulations in a controlled environment. As the rope access industry continues to evolve, new equipment is regularly hitting the market. As these new products are developed, we will continue reviewing options to pursue optimal safety while working at height.

Have fun and keep it safe out there!
