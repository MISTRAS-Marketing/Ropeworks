MISTRAS Ropeworks’ Transition to the Petzl ASAP


Hello Friends,

In January 2012, Petzl released a statement which withdrew support for the use of the Shunt
as a rope access back-up device. The company maintains that when used in this manner the SHUNT can be easily defeated by the user.

Despite using the SHUNT as our primary back-up device for 18 years of incident-free use, we recognized and found merit in Petzl’s concerns and decided it was time to identify another device. In May we transitioned to using the ASAP as the primary back-up device in our rope access training courses. Implementation into our field operations is underway.

We approached this change thoughtfully and deliberately. To help us in the process, we assembled seven of our level-3 Field Team Leaders and Lead Instructors over a weekend in March. The goal was to sample a few devices and identify the one that would offer the best combination of safety and efficiency. After careful deliberation, we identified the Petzl ASAP as a viable option and decided to perform some independent testing.

In April 2012, Petzl America was gracious enough to invite us to Clearfield, UT to perform a series of demonstrations with the ASAP, simulating various rope access scenarios found in field and training environments. We captured the “tests”on camera and in extensive notes. These videos along with informative summaries can be found on our website at the following link:

• ASAP Demonstrations at Petzl America

The data collected cannot be considered scientific but is a compelling demonstration of the ASAP’s consistent performance as well as some limitations. Below is an outline of our general learnings from the testing we’ve done thus far:

• Having the ASAP positioned directly above an obstacle (e.g., I’D, handle ascender or knot)
does not detrimentally affect its function or performance.

• Locking or engaging the ASAP on rope is one way to minimize potential free fall distances
when working statically on rope. This practice, however, is likely to expedite wear. A better
option is to tie a knot (or slip knot) directly below the ASAP to maintain the device in a high
position. In windy conditions, this can also help prevent excessive rope run-out above the
device, causing a large fall-distance hazard.

• Device engagement distance is consistent regardless of the fall factor.

• Overall, fall distance was very consistent and less than other devices we’ve tested.

• When monitored appropriately the ASAP can be connected to the anchor and safely used as a back-up device in a vertical hauling scenario (e.g., pitch-head haul).

• The ASAP is designed to engage at (or just over) 2.5 m/s. It does not protect against low
velocity vertical falls, uncontrolled descent, or low velocity swing falls (low angle horizontal
travel) at speeds below 2.5 m/s. When exposed to these conditions the user must mitigate
the situation appropriately.

• Tying a well placed knot in the safety line below the ASAP is an effective method of mitigating the low velocity swing fall hazard.

• Minimize time spent with two ASAPs connected to the same line (passing an obstacle).

• When using one ASAP per person in a two person load situation, it is optimal to connect to
two individual ropes rather than one.

• Low clearance situations require special attention for all back-up devices. Each scenario is
different and needs to be mitigated appropriately.

• Performing a thorough risk assessment is essential for all rope access equipment.

• Appropriate training is required for the safe use of all rope access equipment.

We were satisfied with the results of our simulated skills test and the drop simulations in a controlled environment. As the rope access industry continues to evolve, new equipment is regularly hitting the market. As these new product are developed, we will continue reviewing options to pursue optimal safety while working at height.

Have fun and keep it safe out there!

Sincerely,
The Ropeworks Team of Mistras Group, Inc.


Additional Note: We would like to thank Petzl for the use of their facility, supply of equipment and collaboration of efforts to perform and document the scenario demonstrations. At the conclusion of our visit to Petzl America we were asked to suggest “improvements” we would like to see to the ASAP. Among a short list of “improvements”, we emphasised the need for a “parking” mode to maintain a high, tensioned position on the safety rope. It was encouraging to see how well the ASAP performed in its current form and that Petzl was interested in addressing needs specific to the rope access industry.
